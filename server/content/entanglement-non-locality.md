Entanglement is the feature of quantum mechanics that most stubbornly resists classical
explanation. When two particles become entangled, their properties are linked so tightly that
the pair must be described by a single shared state, even when the particles are carried light
years apart. Measure one and you instantly learn something about the other, a connection
Einstein famously derided as spooky action at a distance because it seemed to violate the
intuition that objects have definite properties independent of observation.

Einstein's preferred resolution was that the particles secretly carried predetermined answers
all along, hidden variables fixed at the moment they separated, so that no real influence ever
travelled between them. For decades this looked like an untestable matter of taste. Then in 1964
John Bell did something remarkable: he showed that the two worldviews make different, measurable
predictions about how often distant measurement outcomes agree.

![Two linked points of light across space](https://images.pexels.com/photos/957061/milky-way-starry-sky-night-sky-star-957061.jpeg?auto=compress&cs=tinysrgb&w=1200 "Correlations beyond any local story")

Bell's insight was to consider measurements taken along different, randomly chosen settings on
each particle. Any theory in which each particle carries its own local instructions obeys a
ceiling on the strength of the resulting correlations, a limit now called a Bell inequality.
Quantum mechanics, by contrast, predicts correlations that exceed this ceiling for certain
settings. The disagreement is not subtle philosophy; it is a number you can count in a
laboratory.

Experiments began testing Bell inequalities in the 1970s and 1980s, and quantum mechanics won
every time. Skeptics pointed to loopholes: perhaps the detectors were inefficient and sampled a
biased subset, or perhaps the measurement settings were chosen too early to rule out some hidden
communication. Over the following decades, increasingly stringent experiments closed these
loopholes, culminating around 2015 in tests that sealed the major ones simultaneously. The
conclusion is now as firmly established as any in physics: nature is not locally realistic.

It is important to be precise about what this does and does not allow. The correlations are
genuine and stronger than any classical story permits, yet they cannot be used to send a signal
faster than light. The outcome of each individual measurement looks completely random; the
strange agreement only appears when the two parties later compare their results. This no-signalling
property is what keeps entanglement compatible with relativity, frustrating as that balance may
feel.

For quantum computing, entanglement is not a paradox to be explained away but a resource to be
spent. It is what allows a register of qubits to represent and process correlations that scale
beyond anything a classical machine can track efficiently, and it is the active ingredient in
teleportation, superdense coding, and the error-correcting codes that protect fragile quantum
information. A quantum computer without entanglement could be simulated easily by a classical one;
with it, the door opens to genuinely new computational power. Bell's theorem, born as a question
about the foundations of reality, turns out to underwrite the most practical promises of the
field.
